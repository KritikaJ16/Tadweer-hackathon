# Proof of Impact

### Why an idea like Desert Pulse matters — evidence from real communities

Desert Pulse rests on one premise: if you **ask an underserved community what it needs**, turn those answers into **ranked, local business opportunities**, and hand people a **plan to act on them**, real economic and social value follows.

That premise is not just hopeful. Each link in the chain — letting communities name their own priorities, and then standing up the kinds of local services Desert Pulse surfaces — has been studied in the field for decades. This document gathers that evidence so the project can stand on more than a good story.

> **Honest framing:** Desert Pulse is a decision-support and discovery tool, not a guarantee of outcomes. The studies below show that *this category of intervention* works when done well; they don't promise that any single auto-generated plan will succeed. Context, capital, and follow-through still decide everything.

---

## 1. Letting communities identify their own needs works

The core method — surfacing priorities *from residents* rather than imposing them top-down — is the logic behind **Community-Driven Development (CDD)**, one of the most-funded approaches in global development.

- The World Bank has steadily expanded CDD lending specifically to let communities lead their own development, supporting needs the communities themselves identify — water, schools, health facilities, nutrition, and livelihoods. ([World Bank — CDD Results Profile](https://www.worldbank.org/en/results/2013/04/14/community-driven-development-results-profile))
- A 2016 Independent Evaluation Group review found community-led approaches among the **most-used operating models in the World Bank's fragile and conflict-affected portfolio**, valued precisely because they reach remote, hard-to-serve areas quickly and inclusively — partly because community groups often know best what each place actually needs. ([World Bank — CDD Overview](https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/communitydrivendevelopment))

**Takeaway for Desert Pulse:** the instinct to start from a resident's own words — and to weight what's *repeated* across many residents — mirrors a method that institutions have invested in heavily because it travels well to exactly the kind of remote, underserved place Al Qua'a represents.

## 2. The opportunity types Desert Pulse surfaces have documented impact

Desert Pulse's catalogue spans health, transport, agriculture/utilities, women-led services, childcare, and local trades. Here is field evidence that each of these, when it reaches an underserved community, moves real outcomes.

### Mobile health & pharmacy access
- A literature review of U.S. mobile health clinics found they **reach vulnerable populations, adapt to local need, produce cost savings, and improve health outcomes** in underserved groups. ([NIH/PMC — scope & impact review](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5629787/))
- A rural cost study concluded mobile clinics can improve community health and reduce system costs by managing chronic disease and keeping patients engaged in care. ([BMC Health Services Research](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12913-024-12203-5))
- Scale is real: roughly **2,000 mobile clinics** operate in the U.S.; one health system's six units delivered over **12,000 vaccinations to ~8,500 people** during the pandemic. ([ScienceDirect — MHC vaccination case study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666535224000879)) · ([Tulane SPH](https://publichealth.tulane.edu/blog/mobile-health-clinics/))

### Transport & market access
- In Ethiopia, access to paved roads was associated with about an **8% increase in household consumption**, while remoteness depressed it. ([Journal of Economics and Development](https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/jed-10-2022-0213/full/html))
- A review of road improvements in Bangladesh found an overall **~11% rise in per-capita household consumption**, alongside lower input prices and higher farm output. ([UNESCAP — Impacts of rural roads on poverty & equity](https://www.unescap.org/sites/default/files/2.%20Impacts%20of%20rural%20roads%20on%20poverty%20and%20equity.pdf))
- In India, better rural connectivity increased **market integration and availability of non-local goods**, changing how households earn and buy. ([Journal of Development Economics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0304387818300063))

*Relevance:* shared transport and local delivery/marketplace ideas in Desert Pulse attack the same "distance to market and services" barrier these studies measure.

### Solar & water systems for farms
- An Ethiopian study of solar pumps and water-harvesting found measurable gains in **agricultural productivity, income, and food security** for smallholders. ([MDPI Sustainability](https://doi.org/10.3390/su17041486))
- Modelling by IIASA estimated that solar irrigation in sub-Saharan Africa could turn roughly **US$3 billion/year of investment into over US$5 billion/year of added smallholder profit**, plus food-security and energy co-benefits. ([IIASA](https://iiasa.ac.at/news/aug-2023/solar-powered-irrigation-game-changer-for-small-scale-farms-in-sub-saharan-africa))
- A multi-country assessment found that **~43 million households** across 11 countries could benefit from a solar water pump, with closing the gap supporting food security for an estimated **98 million people**. ([CLASP](https://www.clasp.ngo/report/net-zero-heroes/spotlights/spotlight-on-solar-water-pumps/))
- Field work in Kenya linked solar irrigation to **stronger household resilience and faster recovery from shocks**. ([Shell Foundation](https://shellfoundation.org/news/how-solar-irrigation-is-transforming-the-lives-of-smallholder-farmers-in-kenya-lessons-and-the-road-ahead/))

### Women-led home enterprises (bakery, salon & tailoring, childcare)
- In China's Guizhou province, home-based "fingertip economy" work has engaged **more than 500,000 women since 2013**, lifting family income through enterprise done from home. ([ScienceDirect — rural women's entrepreneurship](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S074301672300222X))
- A systematic review of female-led rural micro/nano-enterprises documents income generation and **empowerment gains** — though it stresses these depend on training, market access, and support, not credit alone. ([MDPI — female-led rural nanoenterprises review](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3387/15/8/321))

*Relevance:* Desert Pulse deliberately surfaces low-startup-cost, home-based, women-friendly opportunities (bakery, salon/tailoring, daycare) — the exact category this literature ties to women's economic participation.

## 4. What the evidence does — and doesn't — claim

**It supports:**
- Starting from community-expressed need is a credible, well-funded development method.
- The service categories Desert Pulse ranks have repeatedly produced income, access, and resilience gains in comparable underserved/rural settings.

**It does not claim:**
- That a templated, auto-generated plan equals a validated business. Desert Pulse flags low-evidence ideas as *emerging / needs validation* for this reason.
- That results transfer 1:1 to Al Qua'a. Most cited studies are from other regions; local cost, culture, regulation, and demand still govern outcomes.
- Causation from correlation in every case — several studies note effects vary by gender, capital, and governance.

Desert Pulse's honest role is to **shorten the distance between a community's voice and a credible first plan** — the step the evidence says is worth taking.
